Who's Watching Big Brother?

The Use of Facial Recognition Technology by Law Enforcement and Humanity's Fear of Surveillance

Facial Recognition and the Fight for Justice

I was interested in writing about the ways facial recognition technology is used by law enforcement to advocate for banning its use in Minnesota, a case currently undergoing litigation in the courts. I have been a strong believer in the power of technology my entire life. I have also been concerned with preventing its use from having disparate impacts on marginalized communities.

While one of my early loves may have been computers, I have a far greater love for people and for ensuring they receive justice. The idea that a technology shown to have significant discrepancies could be used to help incarcerate individuals worried me deeply. I did not want to repeat the story of Robert Williams, a Black man who was wrongfully detained after being incorrectly identified by facial recognition technology (FRT) in Michigan. There was a growing uproar about the dangers of artificial intelligence being used by law enforcement and the justice system.

The case, while still valid, wasn’t as strong as I initially believed it to be. Although the technology raises concerns I have ideas to address, it is also true that it helps protect vulnerable people. Biometrics have been used by the police in some form since the early 1900s and have helped build cases against real perpetrators.

To date, there have been 8 people wrongfully accused because of the use of FRT; their stories matter, and receiving justice for every single one of them is something I believe is of paramount importance. A more convincing case for the use of this technology would likely be made by regulating it rather than ruling it out altogether.

Why Do We Fear Surveillance?

I thought I would investigate a question I had been curious about for a while. Oversurveillance is one of the greatest fears people cite as a danger of our increasingly technology-reliant world. But why do we fear surveillance so much? Why are some of the greatest works of modern fiction treatises on the harms of surveillance? - Michel Foucault

To find answers, I turned to philosophy.

In Michel Foucault’s most influential work, Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison, he uses the imagery of the Panopticon, an imposing tower in the center of a prison complex that allows guards to see all criminals at all times, to highlight how the fear of constant surveillance in prisons—and broader society—leads to a kind of self-subjugation. We become our wardens. This has come to be understood as the ‘chilling effect’ of surveillance, something that human rights activists are particularly concerned about.

Why? Well, as it turns out, a great measure of human rights violations is the extent to which people feel the need to self-police. A hallmark of fascist and authoritarian regimes is an ever-present fear of being watched by the powers that be and the behavior that engenders.

In his dystopian novel, 1984, George Orwell’s characters live in a society forced into obedience by the looming shadow of Big Brother, the face of authoritarian government, said to always be watching you through television screens placed everywhere.

In a paper from the Journal of Human Rights Practice, researchers interviewed 44 people from two African countries known for their long-serving dictatorial leaders and their alleged brutal crackdowns of dissent. One of those countries was Uganda, the other was my home country, Zimbabwe.

Researchers cited self-censorship, an unwillingness to engage with individuals or organizations believed to be subject to surveillance, and an erosion of trust, which affected a group’s ability to organize and mobilize, and therefore to be effective politically, as symptoms of the constant surveillance the research participants had experienced. There’s a real fear here that real actors with real power could use the information they have on you to silence and control you—or worse yet, get you to do it to yourself.

Information as a Tool of Power

One of the consequences of the culture of surveillance that Foucault writes about is the importance placed on information. A society designed with a mechanism of control contingent on being privy to large amounts of information is going to privilege information. Some believe that computers were an inevitable development of this kind of society, because as machines, built to be able to store and process tons of information, they become a means to forward the mission of this kind of society.

The Risks of Facial Recognition

This brings us back to the use of FRT by law enforcement. What contemporaneous discussions highlight about the potential use and misuse is the ability for tools like FRT to not only cause harm to society as a whole but to disproportionately cause particular damage to members of society most vulnerable.

FRT’s use by law enforcement began to make headlines when researchers at MIT found that certain algorithms being used had an error rate as low as 0.8% for light-skinned men and as high as 34.7% for darker-skinned women. Given regulations around the technology tend to lag behind its development—as is the case with most modern cutting-edge technology—organizations such as the Minnesota ACLU have had cause for concern with its use, especially as the data stored related to faces is not usually opted into and is not covered by the Government Data Practices Act.

What Would Regulation Look Like?

The harms of unregulated use of FRT are real and frightening, but what might it look like to regulate it?

It would firstly require that the technology be used like all evidence: as helpful to building up a case, but never in and of itself being able to convict anyone. Software Engineering and Data Science Professor at the University of St. Thomas, Dr. Manjeet Rege, believes the technology should be used as an investigative tool rather than a smoking gun.

He argues that the technology has gone past its experimental phase and that much of the concern around accuracy is no longer as founded. The technology does not have perfect accuracy, but neither does witness testimony. The way forward, from his perspective, is to use the technology responsibly, not to discard it altogether.

Dr. Rege’s argument is well-researched and sensible, but what he doesn’t address is the more primal fear the technology poses—the concern that we may be investing in tools of our own self-destruction. Here, other criminal legal experts advocate for independent watchdog groups made up of experts, community leaders, and specialists, the ability for courts to challenge the use of evidence obtained via artificial intelligence, and transparency as likely being stepping stones to build the trust the technology will need to receive broader acceptance.

Until many of these measures are in place, people will continue to ask: If Big Brother is watching us, who’s watching Big Brother?
